From the middle of three flag poles outside the municipal administrative building of the primarily Afro-Caribbean town of Loíza, the flag of Puerto Rico flies at the same height as the American flag that sits to its left.  

Across the street from the building is a plaza where people gather on benches under shady trees draped in vines and moss, listen to music or eat food from one of the cafes next to town hall.

A few blocks away, about 30 adults from Loíza and eastern Puerto Rico learn how to compost and grow organic vegetables. The community garden lies on the banks of the Rio Grande de Loíza, less than half a mile from where the river meets the Atlantic Ocean. 

The garden sits across a small gravel street from a concrete building where a marine biologist and fisherman ties his nets surrounded by paintings of the river.  

This element is visible in fullscreen mode!

Together, the garden and building make up El Ancón, a nonprofit community center that gets its name from a hand-operated barge that once launched at the same spot. The barge, called the Ancón, was once the only way to get from the capital city of San Jaun to the island’s eastern coast. It was a major economic driver for Loíza, and the building housed a restaurant owned by María Luisa Cortijo, a matriarch of the town.

Andres Santos, a lawyer and the executive director of the center, said many people used to take the Ancón across the river to access services in town or to get to school each day. 

But in 1985, a bridge was built to carry cars more quickly and efficiently over the river. Older community members identified the United States Army or its Corps of Engineers as the builders, but like many other bridges on the island, there is no accessible record of its construction.

Cars that once stopped in Loíza now zoom by.

ASIDE OF THE GOVERNMENT STRUCTURES

On an island susceptible to natural disasters, Loíza is a town that’s been hurt by more than just wind, rain and storm surge. 

In 2017, Hurricane Maria swept across Puerto Rico as a Category 4 storm, knocking out the remaining third of the power grid that hadn’t been destroyed by Hurricane Irma two weeks earlier.

The administration of former President Donald Trump delayed for years roughly $20 billion that Congress appropriated to help Puerto Rico recover from the storms. 

Tired of being bypassed and ignored by the U.S. government, the people of Loíza turn to each other. 

Whilepeople in Loíza struggled to get aid after the storms, the El Ancón community center provided solar-powered electricity and food to the community, Santos said.

Loíza always looks inward toward a spirit of self-reliance and entrepreneurship — a hallmark of the small town.

“The government never contributed anything for [the barge] operation,” Díaz said. “And the people, such as the Cortijo family, who are the ones who own that boat, are the ones who took on the task of playing the role that the government had to do for many years.”

As the bridge made movement across the river more convenient, the Ancón wasn’t necessary anymore and halted after decades of service. 

“How painful it is to be the victim of rejection by those who do not love tradition,” wrote one man from Loíza in the late 1980s. 

But in the same building that was once a riverfront restaurant and transportation destination — shuttered by U.S. government actions that moved cars around the town instead of through it — a new community spirit is finding room to grow.

In the garden at El Ancón, Clara Rivera teaches residents of Loíza and nearby municipalities how to till a plot of land so they can plant organic lettuce. Fresh food can be expensive unless you grow it yourself on the island, she said.

“I think that one of the most empowering things for anyone is the ability to produce their own food, completely free of chemicals, completely free of pressure,” Rivera said. “I will grow what I want to grow, when I want to grow and where I want to grow it — and I’m going to eat it, and you don’t have to put a price on it.”

Rivera doesn’t only want to teach people how to farm their own foods, but to take them out of a mindset of resilience and into a mindset of resistance.  

She sees the disasters that strike the island as barriers to push against, not just accept. 

“We’re trying to empower people to get out of that mentality of, you know, ‘Ah, this, okay, you know, this happens every year,’” she said. “It doesn’t have to happen every year.”

“I have seen in the vast majority of work that the communities can get together aside of the governmental structures.”

– Andres Santos, executive director of El Ancón

Rivera is one of many people in Puerto Rico who believes that the challenges they face cannot only be measured by a hurricane category or a grade on the Richter scale. Instead, they see them as direct results of government choices in which they have no say.

“I have seen in the vast majority of work that the communities can get together aside of the governmental structures,” Santos said. “Because the governments are a bad reflection of what we are.”

It was a year before hurricanes Irma and Maria devastated the island that then-President Barack Obama signed the Puerto Rico Oversight, Management, and Economic Stability Act — widely known as PROMESA — which established the Financial Oversight and Management Board that now controls the entire budget of the Puerto Rican elected government.

The act was passed ostensibly to help Puerto Rico restructure what then-Gov. Alejandro García Padilla, called an “unpayable” public debt of more than $70 billion. The United States Government Accountability Office attributed this debt to annual deficits, borrowing to cover those deficits and the territory’s prolonged economic contraction. Others have attributed the debt to government corruption and financial mismanagement.

Though Puerto Rico is no longer legally bankrupt after a federal court confirmed a plan in 2022 to reduce the debt by 80%, the Board’s work also led to austerity measures like slashing funding for the University of Puerto Rico, closing more than 450 schools and reducing employee benefits previously mandated by the territory’s elected government. 

Naming the act “PROMESA,” the spanish word for “promise,” felt like a slap in the face to Judith Berkan, a San Juan attorney who was recently involved in litigation about public records access against the Board.

“That’s not something that Congress could do with the state, put a board like this in place.”

– David Skeel, chairperson of the Financial Management and Oversight Board

“It’s the most disgusting acronym,” she said. 

The Board is composed of seven legal and economic experts appointed over the past eight years. And when the Board controls the budget, it has the ability to challenge policy on issues from public schools to the power grid. 

“My view of that is we are a colonialist imposition,” said David Skeel, the Board’s chairperson. “That’s not something that Congress could do with the state, put a board like this in place.” 

Skeel said that while the Board’s role was only financial, the line was blurry when it came to what power it had to overturn laws passed by the elected Puerto Rican government. 

“I believe that it was necessary — that you don’t get into the kind of crisis that Puerto Rico was in unless your government is deeply dysfunctional, and the government was so dysfunctional, the crisis was so severe that Congress had to do something,” he said. 

For many Puerto Ricans, though, the United States government isn’t the solution to problems within the Puerto Rican government — it’s just another force pushing them down.

A PLACE TO MANIFEST

Israel “Polpy” Díaz, the fisherman whose nets lean against two coolers that span one wall of the small concrete building that once was filled with diners and ticket buyers, was born and raised in Loíza, said that people in the town have been “thrown aside.” 

Loíza was settled in the 16th century by a group of Yoruba people from West Africa who had been enslaved and brought to Puerto Rico against their will. Now, the municipality has the largest proportion of Black residents of any along the northern coast.

“Our people have been oppressed all of our lives,” he said. “Whether it was our culture, or our color. That is a very confusing topic for us as a country.”

He ties the nets himself from nylon filaments, he said — because the best pre-made ones are too expensive to buy. 

“The best quality stops in the United States,” Díaz said. “The second best comes here.”

Díaz is talking about a law passed more than 100 years ago. The Jones Act — which requires that goods transported between two American ports must be transported on ships flying U.S. flags — was passed in the wake of World War I to protect American shipbuilders and boost the maritime economy. 

The legislation prevents foreign goods from reaching Puerto Rico without accruing extra shipping costs along the way.

And not only do goods cost more, but sometimes they don’t even arrive. 

“If the port of Jacksonville is ever closed due to a climate issue, Puerto Rico dies of hunger because it cannot, it cannot receive cargo from anywhere else. And that has practically happened with several hurricanes,” Díaz said.

Citizens of the island don’t vote in federal elections. They are citizens of a United States territory, not a state, and territories don’t have voting representation in Congress or any representation in the Electoral College that elects the president. 

“What we have here is rules from the federal government that we didn’t participate in choosing,” Santos said. 

So instead of waiting for government action, the directors of El Ancón are acting locally. They hope to reopen the barge as a tourist attraction, bring economic growth back to Loíza and encourage people to interact with the community’s history and identity.  

“If I have a seat and if I have food, then you and your loved ones will. And if that multiplies, then we’ll have that chain that’s required to survive.”

– Clara Rivera

Rivera is teaching people how to grow their own food so they can rely on themselves and their communities. 

“If I have a seat and if I have food, then you and your loved ones will,” she said. “And if that multiplies, then we’ll have that chain that’s required to survive.” 

Díaz is helping to plan for the new barge with environmental impacts in mind and wants people to come see it first-hand. 

“I would tell the world to approach this marginalized little town so they get the real impression of what it is,” he said. “They will be a part of it, and we will wait for them with open arms.” 

And Santos is still thinking about how he can begin to build his dreams for Puerto Rico.

“But looking forward, I am in this way very lucky,” he said. “To have a place to manifest what I look forward to for my country in a small space.”
